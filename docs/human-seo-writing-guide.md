# Human-First SEO Writing Guide

> **Purpose:** Every piece of content for chicagocommercialcleaner.com must read like a real Chicago business owner or cleaning industry expert wrote it — not a language model. This guide is the rulebook.

---

## The Core Problem

AI detectors flag content based on three signals:
1. **Low perplexity** — word choices are too predictable, too "correct"
2. **Low burstiness** — sentences are all similar length and rhythm
3. **Pattern stacking** — overuse of the same transitions, structures, and filler phrases

Google's March 2024 Helpful Content Update wiped 45% of low-value sites that published unrefined AI output. The fix isn't "make AI sound human" — it's **write with a real voice, real opinions, and real specifics.**

## Banned Structural Patterns

These sentence/paragraph constructions are AI fingerprints. Avoid all of them.

### 1. "Not X, But Y" Construction
Bad: "It's not just about speed — it's about efficiency."
Good: "Speed matters. Efficiency matters more."

### 2. Tricolons (Rule of Three)
Bad: "Fast, reliable, and affordable."
Good: "Fast and reliable." OR break them into separate sentences.

### 3. Em Dash Overuse
**Max: 1 em dash per 500 words.** AI loves em dashes. Humans use periods.
Bad: "This feature — which changed everything — helped users."
Good: "This feature changed everything. It helped users."

### 4. Rhetorical Question + Immediate Answer
Bad: "What does this mean for you? It means savings."
Good: "This saves you money."

### 5. Parallel Mirror Structure
Bad: "Small businesses need X. Large enterprises need Y."
Good: "Small businesses need X. For larger companies, Y works better."

### 6. Colon Reveals
Bad: "The result: better outcomes."
Good: "The result was better outcomes." Or just state the outcome.

### 7. Neat Paragraph Endings
AI wraps every paragraph up with a tidy conclusion. Humans don't.
**Rule: 30%+ of your paragraphs should just stop. No bow. No summary sentence.**

### 8. Wikipedia Intro Style
Bad: "Commercial cleaning is a professional service that involves..."
Good: Start with the hook, the problem, or the opinion. Never a definition.

## Reading Level & Sentence Rules

**Target: 5th grade reading level.**

- Average sentence length: 12-15 words
- Hard ceiling: no sentence over 25 words
- Short words beat long ones every time
- One idea per sentence

**Test:** Read it out loud. If you pause for breath mid-sentence, it's too long. Split it.

### No Hedging

AI hedges constantly. Humans with expertise don't.

Bad: "This could potentially help you maybe improve..."
Good: "This helps you improve..."

Bad: "Arguably one of the best..."
Good: "One of the best..." or just "The best..."

Bad: "It's worth considering that perhaps..."
Good: (Just state the thing.)

## Voice & Tone Rules

### Write Like a Specific Person
Every blog post is written from the perspective of someone who has actually cleaned Chicago offices. Not a marketer. Not a content writer. A person who knows what it smells like when a Loop high-rise hasn't had its carpets shampooed in 6 months.

**Voice profile:**
- Direct. Says what they mean.
- Knows Chicago — references actual streets, neighborhoods, weather, buildings
- Has opinions. Not afraid to say "most cleaning companies cut corners on baseboards"
- Occasionally blunt. Not rude, but not corporate-smooth either.
- Uses contractions naturally (don't, won't, it's, we've)

### Sentence Rhythm (Burstiness)

AI writes in uniform medium-length sentences. Humans don't.

**Bad (AI-like):**
> Commercial cleaning services are essential for maintaining a healthy workplace. Professional cleaners ensure that your office meets hygiene standards. Regular cleaning schedules help reduce the spread of germs and bacteria. Investing in professional services can improve employee productivity.

**Good (human-like):**
> Your office is dirtier than you think. The keyboard alone has 400x more bacteria than a toilet seat — and that's before flu season hits Chicago in November. We see it every week. Companies wait until half the team calls in sick, then panic-call for a deep clean. By then you've already lost thousands in productivity. A twice-weekly cleaning schedule costs a fraction of that.

Key differences:
- Mix of short punchy sentences (5-8 words) and longer ones (15-25 words)
- Starts with a bold claim, not a definition
- Uses a specific stat with context
- References Chicago + season specifically
- Has a point of view ("we see it every week")
- Ends with a practical comparison, not a vague benefit

### Paragraph Length Variation

- Some paragraphs: 1 sentence.
- Some: 2-3 sentences.
- Occasional longer paragraph: 4-5 sentences max.
- Never write 6+ sentence paragraphs. Break them up.

## Chicago-Specific Writing Rules

Generic content gets outranked. Chicago-specific content doesn't just rank better — it reads as obviously written by someone local.

### Always Include:
- **Real neighborhood names:** The Loop, River North, West Loop, Streeterville, Wicker Park — not just "Chicago"
- **Street references:** "offices along Michigan Ave," "the warehouse district near Cermak"
- **Weather/seasonal tie-ins:** Chicago winter salt tracked into lobbies, summer humidity and mold, spring pollen on windows
- **Local landmarks as reference points:** "two blocks from the Merchandise Mart," "the high-rises along Lake Shore Drive"
- **CTA/L train references:** "our crews service buildings along the Blue Line corridor"
- **Chicago-specific problems:** salt stains on lobby floors (winter), lake-effect dust, construction dust from constant building projects, pests in older buildings

### Never Write:
- Generic city descriptions ("Chicago is a vibrant city known for...")
- Tourist-guide facts ("home to deep-dish pizza and the Bean")
- Chamber of commerce language ("a thriving business community")

## Content Structure That Reads Human

### Intro Pattern (First 100 Words)
Don't start with a definition. Don't start with "In today's..." Don't start with the keyword.

Start with ONE of these:
1. **A specific scenario** — "Last Tuesday, our crew walked into a 30,000 sq ft office in the West Loop. The previous cleaning company had been skipping the break room for weeks."
2. **A surprising stat** — "The average Chicago office worker touches 10 million bacteria before lunch. And 80% of that lives on surfaces your current cleaner probably ignores."
3. **A direct opinion** — "Most commercial cleaning companies in Chicago are quoting you for the bare minimum. Here's how to tell."
4. **A question that isn't rhetorical garbage** — "What would happen if the health inspector walked into your restaurant kitchen right now?"

### Body Content Rules
- **Lead with the answer, not the buildup.** If someone searches "how much does office cleaning cost in Chicago," the first sentence should contain a price range.
- **Use specific numbers.** Not "affordable prices" — say "$0.15-$0.35 per square foot." Not "years of experience" — say "since 2019."
- **Include at least one thing you'd only know from doing the work.** Example: "Elevator lobbies in River North high-rises need daily attention because of foot traffic from the L — we see 3x the dirt compared to suburban offices."
- **Break up walls of text** with subheadings that are actual useful phrases, not clever/cute headers.

### Closing Pattern
Don't end with:
- ~~"In conclusion..."~~
- ~~"Ready to get started? Contact us today!"~~
- ~~Restating everything you just said~~

Do end with:
- A practical next step: "Get a quote for your building — most take under 2 minutes and we'll include a walkthrough."
- A honest take: "Not every office needs a cleaning service. If you're under 2,000 sq ft, a weekly deep clean might be all you need."
- A local callback: "We're cleaning offices in the Loop, West Loop, and River North this week. If your building is nearby, we can usually do a same-day walkthrough."

## Editing AI Drafts Into Human Content

When using AI to draft content, follow this process:

### Step 1: Strip the AI Skeleton
- Delete the intro paragraph entirely. Write a new one from scratch.
- Delete the conclusion. Write a new one.
- Remove all transitional phrases between sections.

### Step 2: Inject Specifics
- Replace every generic claim with a Chicago-specific example
- Add a real scenario or anecdote (even a composite one based on real situations)
- Insert exact numbers where the draft says "many" or "significant" or "often"

### Step 3: Break the Rhythm
- Find the longest sentence in each section. Split it.
- Find three consecutive medium sentences. Make one of them very short.
- Add a one-sentence paragraph somewhere unexpected.

### Step 4: Add Opinion
- Find at least 2 places where the draft is neutral/safe. Take a stance.
- Example: Change "There are many cleaning companies to choose from" → "Most cleaning companies in Chicago will quote you low and cut corners. Check if they clean baseboards — 80% don't unless you specifically ask."

### Step 5: Final Read
- Read the whole piece aloud. Mark every spot where you'd never actually say that sentence out loud. Rewrite those.
